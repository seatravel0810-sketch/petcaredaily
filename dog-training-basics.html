How to Train a Dog to Sit, Stay, and Come (Step-by-Step)

✍️ Mike Torres, CPDT-KA 📅 June 15, 2026 ⏱️ 9 min read
🎓

Teaching your dog the basics — sit, stay, and come — is the foundation of a well-behaved, safe dog. These three commands alone can prevent accidents, keep your dog safe in dangerous situations, and make every day with your dog more enjoyable.

The good news: any dog can learn these commands. Even older dogs can master new behaviors with consistent, positive reinforcement training.

The Training Fundamentals

Before teaching any command, understand these principles that will make your training 10x more effective:

  • Positive reinforcement only — Reward the behavior you want, ignore the behavior you don't. Never punish during training.
  • Short sessions — 5–10 minutes per session, 2–3 times per day. Dogs lose focus quickly.
  • High-value treats — Use tiny (pea-sized) pieces of real meat, cheese, or commercial training treats
  • Marker word or clicker — "Yes!" or a clicker sound marks the exact moment of correct behavior
  • End on success — Always end the session with a command your dog knows well and can succeed at

Teaching "Sit" — The First Command

"Sit" is typically the easiest command to teach and makes a great starting point for all training.

Step-by-Step:

  1. Stand in front of your dog with a treat in your hand
  2. Hold the treat close to your dog's nose, then slowly move your hand up — their head will follow the treat, causing their bottom to lower
  3. The moment their bottom hits the floor, say "Yes!" and give the treat
  4. Repeat 5–10 times per session
  5. After 2–3 sessions, add the verbal cue "Sit" just before luring
  6. After another 5–10 sessions, try saying "Sit" without the lure — reward heavily when they do it
⏱️ Timeline: Most dogs learn "sit" within 1–3 days. By one week, your dog should sit reliably on a verbal cue alone.

Teaching "Stay" — Building Duration

"Stay" is an extension of "Sit." Only introduce it once your dog sits reliably on cue.

Step-by-Step:

  1. Ask your dog to sit
  2. Hold your palm out flat toward your dog (like a stop signal) and say "Stay"
  3. Wait just 1–2 seconds, then say "Yes!" and reward
  4. Gradually increase the duration — 3 seconds, 5 seconds, 10 seconds — before rewarding
  5. Once your dog can stay for 10 seconds, start adding distance: take one step back, return, reward
  6. Slowly increase both duration and distance over multiple sessions

Three D's of Stay:

  • Duration — How long they stay (build first)
  • Distance — How far you move away (build second)
  • Distraction — Maintaining stay despite distractions (build last)
  • Only work on one "D" at a time — don't increase all three simultaneously

Teaching "Come" — The Most Important Command

"Come" (also called recall) is arguably the most important command you can teach. A reliable recall can save your dog's life if they run toward a road or dangerous situation.

Step-by-Step:

  1. Start in a small, enclosed area with no distractions
  2. Crouch down and open your arms — use happy, exciting body language
  3. Say "Come!" in a happy, high-pitched voice (one time only)
  4. When your dog reaches you, grab their collar gently and reward with your highest-value treats
  5. Make "come" the most exciting thing that ever happens to your dog
  6. Gradually add distance and distractions as they improve
🚨 Critical Rule: NEVER punish your dog when they come to you — even if they were doing something wrong before coming. If coming to you results in anything negative (scolding, bath, nail trim, leash going on), your dog will start avoiding recall. Always make "come" the best thing ever.

Common Training Mistakes to Avoid

  • Repeating commands — Say the command once. Repeating teaches your dog to wait for multiple cues.
  • Training when frustrated — Dogs pick up on your emotional state. End the session if you're getting frustrated.
  • Too long sessions — 5–10 minutes is ideal. Longer sessions cause mental fatigue and reduced learning.
  • Skipping the basics — Don't advance to "stay" until "sit" is solid. Build on success.
  • Inconsistent cues — All family members must use the same words and hand signals.

What to Do If Your Dog Isn't Getting It

If your dog seems stuck, it usually means one of three things:

  • The treats aren't motivating enough — try real cooked chicken or cheese
  • You advanced too quickly — go back to an easier step they can succeed at
  • The environment is too distracting — train in a quieter space first

If your dog is easily distracted, disinterested in food, or shows other concerning behavior during training, consult your veterinarian — sometimes attention issues have underlying medical causes.
