7 Warning Signs Your Cat Is Sick (Don't Ignore These)

✍️ Dr. James Cooper, DVM 📅 June 8, 2026 ⏱️ 6 min read
⚠️

Cats are masters at hiding illness. This survival instinct — inherited from their wild ancestors — means that by the time a cat appears sick, the condition may already be serious. As a cat owner, knowing the subtle signs of illness can literally save your cat's life.

Here are the 7 warning signs every cat owner must know.

1. Changes in Eating or Drinking Habits

A sudden decrease or increase in food or water consumption is one of the earliest and most reliable signs of illness in cats.

  • Eating less for more than 24 hours — Could indicate dental pain, nausea, kidney disease, or depression
  • Sudden increased appetite — May signal hyperthyroidism or diabetes
  • Drinking much more water than usual — Classic sign of kidney disease or diabetes — see a vet within 24–48 hours
💡 Track it: If you measure how much food your cat eats each day, you'll notice changes much earlier. A kitchen scale for food portions is a simple but powerful health monitoring tool.

2. Litter Box Changes

A healthy cat uses the litter box regularly with normal urine output and formed stools. Watch for:

  • Straining to urinate or produce only small amounts — Emergency in male cats — can be fatal within hours
  • Blood in urine or stool
  • Diarrhea lasting more than 24 hours
  • Not using the litter box at all
  • Unusual odor from waste
🚨 Emergency: A male cat that cannot urinate is a life-threatening emergency. Go to an emergency vet immediately — urinary blockages can be fatal within 24–48 hours.

3. Changes in Grooming

Cats spend 30–50% of their waking hours grooming. Sudden changes in this behavior signal something is wrong:

  • Over-grooming or bald patches — May indicate stress, allergies, or parasites
  • Matted, unkempt fur — Often seen in arthritic cats who can't reach to groom, or in cats that are ill
  • Excessive shedding with skin irritation — Could indicate thyroid issues or nutritional deficiency

4. Behavioral Changes

You know your cat better than anyone. Trust your instincts when something seems "off."

  • Hiding more than usual — One of the most common signs cats are unwell
  • Sudden aggression — Often a sign of pain; a normally gentle cat that hisses when touched may be hurting
  • Excessive vocalization — Especially in senior cats; can indicate cognitive dysfunction, hyperthyroidism, or high blood pressure
  • Decreased interaction — Withdrawal from favorite people or activities

5. Respiratory Changes

Cats should breathe quietly and effortlessly. Any of the following warrant immediate attention:

  • Open-mouth breathing (except briefly after intense exercise)
  • Rapid or labored breathing
  • Persistent coughing or sneezing
  • Wheezing or clicking sounds when breathing
  • Blue or gray gums (emergency — call vet immediately)

6. Changes in Eyes, Ears, or Nose

The face can reveal a lot about your cat's health:

  • Third eyelid visible — The pink membrane showing in the corner of the eye often indicates illness or dehydration
  • Cloudy eyes — Can indicate cataracts or hypertension
  • Ear odor or dark discharge — Usually ear mites or infection
  • Persistent nasal discharge — Upper respiratory infection or polyps

7. Weight Changes

Cats can lose weight rapidly when ill, and even a small percentage of body weight loss is significant. Weigh your cat monthly using a baby scale or by weighing yourself holding your cat.

  • 10% weight loss = veterinary attention needed
  • 20% weight loss = serious concern requiring urgent care

Unexplained weight gain, particularly around the abdomen, may indicate fluid buildup (ascites) related to heart disease or cancer.

When to Call the Vet Immediately

These symptoms require emergency veterinary care — don't wait:

  • 🚨 Cannot urinate (especially male cats)
  • 🚨 Difficulty breathing
  • 🚨 Collapse or extreme weakness
  • 🚨 Seizures
  • 🚨 Suspected poisoning
  • 🚨 Severe trauma

When in doubt, call your vet or an emergency animal hospital. Cats deteriorate quickly, and early treatment leads to better outcomes and lower costs.
