10 Signs Your Dog Is in Pain (And What To Do)

✍️ Dr. Emily Reeves, DVM 📅 June 7, 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read
😣

Dogs are stoic animals that often hide their pain — a behavior inherited from their wild ancestors, where showing weakness could make them vulnerable to predators. This instinct means that by the time your dog shows obvious signs of pain, they may have been suffering for quite some time.

Knowing the subtle signs of pain in dogs is one of the most important skills a dog owner can develop.

1. Changes in Posture or Movement

Pain often first shows up in how a dog holds their body and moves:

  • Hunched back or tucked abdomen (abdominal pain)
  • Head held lower than normal
  • Reluctance to go up stairs, jump on furniture, or get in the car
  • Stiff gait, especially when first getting up
  • Limping or favoring one leg

2. Excessive Licking, Chewing, or Scratching

When a dog obsessively licks, chews, or scratches one area of their body, they're usually trying to address pain or discomfort in that area. Check the skin underneath — you may find a wound, hot spot, or swelling.

3. Changes in Sleep Patterns

A dog in pain may sleep more than usual (pain is exhausting), or alternatively may have trouble getting comfortable and sleep less. Watch for your dog shifting positions frequently during the night.

4. Loss of Appetite

Pain is a major cause of appetite loss in dogs. If your dog skips more than one meal, especially combined with other signs of pain, contact your veterinarian.

5. Vocalization

While dogs don't vocalize pain as often as we might expect, some signs include:

  • Yelping or crying when touched in a specific area
  • Whimpering or whining without obvious cause
  • Growling when approached (defensive due to pain)

6. Behavioral Changes

Pain changes personality. A normally friendly dog who suddenly becomes irritable or snappy is often trying to protect a painful area. Other behavioral signs include:

  • Hiding or avoiding interaction
  • Unusual clinginess
  • Decreased interest in play or walks
  • Depression or flat affect
⚠️ Warning: A dog in pain may bite even if they have never shown aggression before. Always approach a potentially painful dog carefully and calmly, and warn others.

7. Panting and Restlessness

Excessive panting when it's not hot and your dog hasn't exercised is a classic sign of pain, anxiety, or illness. Combined with restlessness — not being able to settle or get comfortable — this is a red flag that warrants veterinary attention.

8. Changes in Eyes

Eyes can be windows to pain:

  • Squinting or partially closed eyes
  • Redness or visible third eyelid
  • Pupils that are larger or smaller than normal
  • Glassy or glazed appearance

9. Muscle Tremors or Shaking

Shivering in a dog that isn't cold can indicate pain, nausea, or serious illness. Muscle tremors specifically can indicate neurological pain or severe discomfort.

10. Changes in Breathing

Pain can affect breathing patterns:

  • Shallow, fast breathing at rest
  • Breathing with visible effort
  • Guarding the chest area when touched

What To Do If Your Dog Is in Pain

Never give human pain medications to dogs. Common human painkillers — including ibuprofen (Advil), acetaminophen (Tylenol), and naproxen (Aleve) — are toxic to dogs and can be fatal.

Safe steps to take:

  • Call your veterinarian for guidance
  • Keep your dog calm and restricted from activity
  • Apply cold packs (not ice) to acute injuries for 15 minutes at a time
  • Consider CBD oil for mild chronic pain — it has a good safety profile for dogs
