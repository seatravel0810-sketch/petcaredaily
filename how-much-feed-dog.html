How Much Should You Feed Your Dog Per Day?

✍️ Dr. Emily Reeves, DVM 📅 June 6, 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read
🍽️

Overfeeding is the #1 nutritional mistake dog owners make. According to the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention, over 56% of US dogs are overweight or obese — and most owners don't even realize it. Knowing exactly how much to feed your dog can add years to their life.

Here's a complete, vet-reviewed guide to dog feeding portions for every size and life stage.

The Golden Rule: Follow the Food Label, Then Adjust

Every dog food includes a feeding guide on the packaging based on your dog's weight. These are starting points — not exact prescriptions. You'll need to adjust based on:

  • Your dog's body condition (are they too thin, ideal, or overweight?)
  • Activity level (sedentary house dog vs. working dog)
  • Whether they're spayed/neutered (reduces calorie needs by ~20%)
  • Age (puppies need more; seniors usually need less)
  • Health conditions (thyroid issues, diabetes affect metabolism)

Puppy Feeding Guide

Puppies have very different needs than adult dogs — they need more protein, fat, and calories relative to their body weight, but they also need to eat smaller, more frequent meals since their stomachs are small.

  • 8–12 weeks: 4 meals per day
  • 3–6 months: 3 meals per day
  • 6–12 months: 2 meals per day
  • 12+ months (small breeds): 2 meals per day
  • 12–18 months (large breeds): Transition to adult food

Puppy food should be labeled "for puppies" or "for all life stages." Large breed puppies specifically need food with controlled calcium levels to prevent bone growth disorders.

Senior Dog Feeding Adjustments

Dogs 7+ years old typically need 20–30% fewer calories than adult dogs, but they still need high-quality protein to maintain muscle mass. Watch for:

  • Reduced activity → reduce portions by 10–20%
  • Muscle loss → maintain or increase protein (not total calories)
  • Joint issues → look for food with glucosamine and chondroitin
  • Kidney disease → vet-prescribed low-phosphorus diet

How to Tell If You're Feeding the Right Amount

The best tool is the Body Condition Score (BCS) — a 9-point scale used by vets. Here's a quick home check:

  • Feel the ribs: You should feel them easily without pressing hard, but not see them
  • Waist check from above: Your dog should have a visible waist — an hourglass shape when viewed from above
  • Tuck check from the side: The belly should tuck up slightly behind the ribs, not hang down
💡 Simple Test: Run your fingers along your dog's ribs. If you can't feel them, your dog is overweight. If you can see them clearly, your dog may be underweight. You should feel them like the back of your hand — with slight resistance.

How Often Should You Feed Your Dog?

Most adult dogs do best with 2 meals per day — morning and evening. This is better than once-a-day feeding because it:

  • Reduces the risk of bloat (GDV), especially in large breeds
  • Maintains more stable blood sugar levels
  • Makes it easier to notice when your dog skips a meal (a health signal)

Free-feeding (leaving food out all day) is not recommended for most dogs, as it leads to overeating and makes it impossible to monitor appetite changes.
